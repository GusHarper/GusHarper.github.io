Is the U.K Spending Enough on Education?

Aims

Education is a powerful agent of change, providing individuals with the skill-set and adaptability to improve their standard of living while contributing to social stability and long-term economic growth. This project aims to provide a background on educational spending in the UK, compare its investment with that of other nations, and assess whether investment in the UK has been sufficient.

Background / Graphs

However, spending on education in the UK falls short of nations including Sweden, the United States, and Canada. The World Bank API only provided data for individual countries and series at a time, so a series of loops were used in Collaboratory to collect and clean data. While data from 2017 was the most complete, missing values still plagued the data set. Another issue was that net tertiary enrolment data was unavailable, so gross values were used instead. Expenditure per pupil by LA Maintained Schools fell drastically from 2012-2017 after peaking in 2010, while recent figures from 2019 have indicated expenditure may be picking back up again. Data was taken from yearly expenditure reports from HM Treasury, and was rebased using both a general inflation CPI, and an education-specific inflation CPI, taken from the ONS. Extracting data from the API, along with rebasing, was done in Collaboratory. Initially I was unable to extract specific data from the API in it's json format, however was able to later overcome this by converting to a python dictionary.

To justify the importance of examining historical spending, the analysis is extended to show correlations between spending and educational attainment and economic growth. The following regression indicates a positive correlation between economic growth and spending on education, with a marginal effect of around 0.12. Data was taken from The World Bank. One issue with the regression was reverse causality, as economic growth could cause increases in expenditure, and vice versa. To address this, a 10-year lag was implemented manually. Additionally, the years 2008-2011 we excluded to avoid bias from the financial crisis.

To illustrate the effects of increased spending on educational attainment, a natural experiment was used. In 1993, Massachusetts announced an Education Reform Act that promised increased spending on primary and secondary education, while neighboring states Maine and CT did not. The first graph uses Difference-In-Difference techniques to show the impact of the bill on revenues per pupil, as shown by the dashed yellow line ($858 lower). The second graph illustrates how following a 7-year lag (educational attainment measured at 25, 7 years after graduation), Massachusetts experienced a significant increase in educational attainment from this increased revenues per pupil. Data availability was an issue for these graphs, as the NCES and U.S. Census Bureau provide figures dating back to only 1990. Additionally, measurement techniques changed after 2006, making analysis beyond this period difficult. The difference in difference analysis was done in Stata.

It is useful to examine productivity growth in the UK, as it has slowed drastically over the last several years while aggregate levels also lag behind that of Germany, the U.S, and France. Education is highly related to productivity levels, so this data could indicate higher investment is needed. Data in the CSV file for this graph had to be converted to a tidy format (stacked), as initially only a single country was shown.

Conclusions

Spending on Education in the UK has fallen drastically over the last decade, and has only recently started to recover. As countries with higher expenditure on education typically also experience higher growth rates and average educational attainment, it is undoubtedly of great interest for the UK to increase spending. However, it is difficult to draw conclusions and make recommendations given budget constraints, rising inflation, and increasing healthcare costs from the global pandemic and rising share of the population aged over 65. Nevertheless, the UK should look to marginally increase its spending in the coming years to maintain the upward trajectory seen recently in several key measures, promoting sustained and inclusive economic growth.
